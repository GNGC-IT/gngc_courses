# Mechanical Advantage

**Focus:** Physical build, structural integrity, and motion efficiency.

Mechanical advantage is a measure of the force amplification achieved by using a tool, mechanical device or machine system. The device trades off input forces against movement to obtain a desired amplification in the output force. The model for this is the law of the lever. 

>[!tip]- Law of the Lever?
>It's an Archemedies thing. Check it out here: [Wikipedia - Levers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lever#Law_of_the_lever)

Machine components designed to manage forces and movement in this way are called mechanisms. An ideal mechanism transmits power without adding to or subtracting from it. This means the ideal machine does not include a power source, is frictionless, and is constructed from rigid bodies that do not deflect or wear. The performance of a real system relative to this ideal is expressed in terms of efficiency factors that take into account departures from the ideal.

**Key Aspects of Mechanical Advantage:**

- **Types:**
    - **Ideal Mechanical Advantage (IMA):** Theoretical maximum with zero friction.
    - **Actual Mechanical Advantage (AMA):** Real-world ratio taking friction into account.
<br>
- **Examples of Usage:** Using a crowbar to pry a nail, a lever to move a heavy rock, a wheelbarrow to carry heavy soil, or a pulley system to hoist heavy engine parts.
- **Synonyms/Related Terms:** Force multiplier, leverage, force amplification, machine efficiency (closely related), transmission ratio.

## Gears!

![[gearup_down.png]]

### Speed vs Torque

The main purpose of gears is to alter the energy transferred from your motor in one of two ways: _more torque or more speed. (sorry, you can't really improve both, it's always a trade off)_

The easy way to remember it is:
- If you drive a large gear with a small gear, you increase torque but decrease speed.
- Drive a small gear with a large gear and it's the inverse. _More speed, less torque._

Now, what is the difference between the two? Speed is obvious, but torque is what you need to **move**. The heavier the object you want to move or steeper the incline you need to climb, the more torque you need. Think of a bike. You're on the downhill, lots of speed so you gear up to the smaller gears and get that speed! On the uphill, you need the torque to help push you up the hill, so gear down and get that extra power push. You're never completely trading one property for the other, you're always left with a little of each. It's balancing the trade off for your build that informs your gear decisions.

Gears can sometimes be hidden in mechanisms, so often it's also useful to think of the axles (which are often what transfers the energy from the gear) as the input axle and output axle.

### Ratios?

Gearing up or down is usually represented by a ratio. 

![[gear_ratio.png]]

The left value is always the input: your driver gear. The right value is your output or "follower". These values are determined by the 'teeth' of the gear.

![[lego_teeth.jpeg]]

Here is an example of the lego gears and their various teeth. Lets use an example of attaching an **8 tooth gear** as my **driver gear** and a **16 tooth gear** as my follower.

That would be a 8:16 ratio, or if we math: 1:2.

Small gear as the driver, bigger gear as the follower. Hello more torque!

That's the basic introduction to gears, but there is a lot more to go into for advanced gearing techniques. I'll leave that to you to research [further](https://gearsolutions.com/features/an-advanced-approach-to-optimal-gear-design/).
